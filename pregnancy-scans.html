Research study suggests that pregnancy ultrasound is safe for the unborn child, unlike radiographs, which use ionizing radiation. Real-time scanners form a constant photo of the moving fetus on a display screen. Very high frequency sound waves of in between 3.5 to 7.0 megahertz are generally used for this function. They are launched from a transducer, which is positioned in contact with the maternal abdomen, and is walked around to "" take a look at"" any specific element of the uterus.

Recurring ultrasound beams scan the fetus and are shown back onto the same transducer. The information acquired from different reflections is recomposed back into a picture on the display screen. The sex of the infant can generally be figured out after 16 weeks, relying on the sonographic device and ability of the operator. Movements such as fetal heart beat and malformations in the fetus can be gauged and measurements can be made accurately on the images showed on the screen. Structural irregularities in the fetus can be dependably diagnosed by an ultrasound scan, and these can normally be performed prior to 20 weeks.
